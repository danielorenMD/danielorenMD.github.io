Lipoprotein(a): The Risk Factor We Could Never Treat — Until Now

July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

For decades, cardiovascular medicine has organized itself around a small set of modifiable risk factors: LDL cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes. We measure them, we treat them, and outcomes improve. But there is one powerful, common, and largely inherited risk factor that has sat outside this framework — visible on a blood test, strongly tied to disease, and until very recently, completely untreatable. That factor is lipoprotein(a), usually written Lp(a).

What Lp(a) actually is

Lp(a) is an LDL-like particle with an extra protein, apolipoprotein(a), wrapped around it. That added protein gives Lp(a) two problematic personalities at once. Like LDL, it deposits cholesterol into the arterial wall and drives atherosclerosis. But apolipoprotein(a) also structurally resembles plasminogen, a key player in breaking down clots, and appears to interfere with normal fibrinolysis. So a single particle contributes to both the buildup of plaque and the tendency of blood to clot on top of it — a combination that helps explain why Lp(a) is linked to heart attacks, strokes, and calcific aortic valve disease.

Why it matters more than most people realize

Elevated Lp(a) is common. Roughly one in five people worldwide carries a level high enough to raise cardiovascular risk, and the trait is strongly genetic — your level is set largely by the LPA gene and stays fairly stable across your lifetime. It does not respond meaningfully to diet, exercise, or the lifestyle changes we normally recommend. Someone can do everything right and still carry a substantial, invisible risk burden from a single number they have never had measured.

That last point is the frustrating part. Large genetic and epidemiological studies have made the association between high Lp(a) and cardiovascular events about as robust as anything in the field. Yet the majority of patients — even many with premature heart disease — have never had it checked.

The case for measuring Lp(a) at least once in every adult's lifetime is now strong. It is inherited, stable, and it reclassifies risk in ways nothing else on the standard panel can.

The treatment gap

Here is the paradox that has defined Lp(a) for years: we could identify high-risk patients, but we could not do much about the number itself. Statins, the backbone of lipid-lowering therapy, do not lower Lp(a) and may nudge it slightly upward. PCSK9 inhibitors lower it modestly, but that reduction is a side effect rather than the point, and it is not enough for people with very high levels. Apheresis — physically filtering the blood — works but is invasive, expensive, and impractical at scale.

So the standard clinical advice became indirect: if a patient has high Lp(a), treat everything else more aggressively. Push LDL lower, control blood pressure tightly, address every other risk factor. Reasonable, but unsatisfying. It is the medical equivalent of knowing exactly where the leak is and being told to bail faster.

What's changing now

The most exciting shift is that we can finally target Lp(a) directly. A new generation of therapies uses RNA-based approaches — antisense oligonucleotides and small interfering RNA — to shut down production of apolipoprotein(a) in the liver. Instead of clearing the particle after it forms, these drugs reduce how much of it the body makes in the first place. In early and mid-stage trials, several of these agents have lowered Lp(a) by 80 to 90 percent or more, a magnitude of effect we simply have not had before.

The crucial caveat is that lowering a biomarker is not the same as preventing events. The field has been burned before by treatments that moved a number without improving outcomes. That is why the large cardiovascular outcomes trials now underway matter so much: they are testing whether dramatically lowering Lp(a) actually translates into fewer heart attacks and strokes. If they read out positive, Lp(a) will move from an untreatable curiosity to a modifiable target — and the argument for universal screening becomes almost unavoidable.

What I tell patients today

Even before those trials report, there is a practical takeaway. Measuring Lp(a) once is cheap and informative. A high value should sharpen how aggressively we manage the risk factors we can already treat, and it flags people who may benefit from the new therapies the moment the outcome data support them. A normal value is reassuring and never needs repeating. Either way, it is information worth having.

Lp(a) is a good reminder that medicine advances unevenly. We can spend years able to see a problem clearly while lacking any tool to fix it — and then, sometimes quite suddenly, the tool arrives. We may be at exactly that inflection point now.
